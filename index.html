What is an Orthogonal Trajectory?

In mathematics, an orthogonal trajectory is a curve that intersects another family of curves at right angles (90 degrees).

If you have a family of curves defined by an equation, the orthogonal trajectories are curves that cut across each member of the original family at a perpendicular angle.

Orthogonal trajectories have important applications in physics and engineering, including:

  • Electric field lines and equipotential surfaces
  • Heat flow and isothermal lines
  • Fluid flow and streamlines
  • Optics and wave propagation

Example: Circles and Lines

In this visualization, we show a simple example of orthogonal trajectories:

Original family of curves: x² + y² = r² (circles centered at origin)
Orthogonal trajectories: y = mx (straight lines through origin)

Each straight line intersects each circle at a 90° angle. At every intersection point, the tangent to the circle is perpendicular to the line.

To find orthogonal trajectories mathematically:

  1. Start with the differential equation of the original family
  2. Replace dy/dx with -dx/dy (the negative reciprocal)
  3. Solve the new differential equation to get the orthogonal family
